Why most apps fail in Grade 5 ESL classrooms

Most "free vocabulary apps" were built for adult solo learners (Duolingo, Memrise, Anki). When you hand them to a Grade 5 ESL class you hit the same walls every time:

  • Sign-up friction. Email required, parental consent required, password resets — tedious for a 32-student class.
  • Adult content depth. Lessons that aim at adult travellers + business English, not 10-year-olds learning fruit names.
  • Solo-loop, not classroom-loop. No way for a teacher to assign 30 specific words this week and see who mastered them.
  • Ads or paywalls. 12-year-old phones see ads or hit a payment screen mid-lesson.

What actually works for 10–12 year olds

  1. Visual + audio for every word. Grade 5 students learn faster from a picture + spoken word than from a written definition.
  2. Short, fast game rounds. 60-second matching, speed quiz, scramble — not 20-minute lessons.
  3. Live class competition. Leaderboards turn passive practice into peer-engagement.
  4. Per-word mastery tracking. A teacher can see exactly which 5 words a student keeps failing and re-assign just those.
  5. Zero sign-up friction. A class code + a name. No email, no password.
  6. Hebrew / Arabic / mother-tongue support — vital for ESL classes where students need to confirm meaning quickly.

How many vocabulary words should a Grade 5 ESL student know?

Roughly 800–1,500 words by the end of Grade 5, depending on the curriculum and how many years of English the student has had. That maps to CEFR A1 → mid-A2. For a student who started English in Grade 1, the realistic target by Grade 5 is solid A1 with the start of A2. For a student who started in Grade 4, expect strong pre-A1 → entering A1.
